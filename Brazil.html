Brazil

Introduction

Brazil, country of South America that occupies half the continent’s landmass. It is the fifth largest country in the world, exceeded in size only by Russia, Canada, China, and the United States, though its area is greater than that of the 48 conterminous U.S. states. Brazil faces the Atlantic Ocean along 4,600 miles (7,400 km) of coastline and shares more than 9,750 miles (15,700 km) of inland borders with every South American country except Chile and Ecuador specifically, Uruguay to the south; Argentina, Paraguay, and Bolivia to the southwest; Peru to the west; Colombia to the northwest; and Venezuela, Guyana, Suriname, and French Guiana to the north. Brazil stretches roughly 2,700 miles (4,350 km) from north to south and from east to west to form a vast irregular triangle that encompasses a wide range of tropical and subtropical landscapes, including wetlands, savannas, plateaus, and low mountains. Brazil contains most of the Amazon River basin, which has the world’s largest river system and the world’s most-extensive virgin rainforest. The country contains no desert, high-mountain, or arctic environments.

Culture

The cultures of the Indigenous, Africans, and Portuguese have together formed the modern Brazilian way of life. The Portuguese culture is by far the dominant of these influences; from it Brazilians acquired their language, their main religion, and most of their customs. The Indigenous population is now statistically small, but Tupí-Guaraní, the language of many Indigenous Brazilians, continues to strongly influence the Brazilian Portuguese language; other Indigenous contributions to Brazilian culture are most apparent in the Amazon basin. African influences on the Brazilian way of life are strongest along the coast between the Northeast and Rio de Janeiro; they include traditional foods, religions, and popular music and dance, especially the samba. Commercial and cultural imports from Europe and North America have often competed with—and influenced—Brazilians’ own cultural output, and critics have argued that the nation’s cultural identity is suffering as a result. Despite numerous social and economic challenges, Brazilians continue to be exuberant and creative in their celebrations and art forms.

Carnival, the four day pre-Lenten Carnival is the most famous and exuberant Brazilian holiday. Carnival in Brazil is the traditional combination of a Roman Catholic festival with the lively celebrations of people of African ancestry. It evolved principally in urban coastal areas, notably in the former plantation zones along the coast between Recife and Rio de Janeiro.

Food/Cuisine

Feijoada is considered Brazil’s national dish. It is a slow cooked stew made with black beans and different cuts of pork or beef. It is usually served with rice, collard greens, orange slices, and farofa, which is toasted cassava flour.

Pão de queijo are small cheese bread rolls that are crispy on the outside and soft inside. They are made with tapioca flour, which gives them a chewy texture. They are commonly eaten for breakfast or as a snack.

Coxinha is a popular street food snack shaped like a teardrop. It is made of shredded chicken wrapped in dough, then breaded and fried. It is crunchy on the outside and soft inside.

Brigadeiro is a traditional chocolate dessert made with condensed milk, cocoa powder, and butter. The mixture is rolled into small balls and covered with chocolate sprinkles. It is very common at parties and celebrations.

Farofa is toasted cassava flour mixed with butter, onions, and sometimes eggs or meat. It is served as a side dish and adds crunch and flavor to meals like feijoada.

Moqueca (labeled as Brigadeiro in original text) is a flavorful seafood stew made with fish or shrimp, coconut milk, tomatoes, onions, and peppers. It is usually cooked slowly and served with rice. There are different regional versions across Brazil.

Açaí na tigela is a cold dish made from frozen açaí berries blended into a thick puree. It is often topped with fruit, granola, or honey. It is popular as a refreshing and energizing food.

History

North America’s first humans migrated from Asia, presumably over a now-submerged land bridge from Siberia to Alaska sometime about 12,000 years ago, during the last Ice Age; it has also been argued, however, that some people arrived earlier, possibly up to 60,000 years ago. Unknown numbers of people moved southward along the western edge of the North American ice cap. The presence of the ice, which for a time virtually covered Canada, makes it reasonable to assume that the southern reaches of North America were settled before Canada, and that the Inuit who live in Canada’s Arctic regions today were the last of the aboriginal peoples to reach Canada. There is general agreement that Native American peoples are related to Asian peoples and that the closest resemblances are between North American Arctic peoples and their counterparts in Siberia.

Sights/Attractions

Christ the Redeemer

Christ the Redeemer is a gigantic statue of Jesus Christ that stands 30 meters tall on top of Mount Corcovado. It is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World and a symbol of Brazil. From the top, visitors can see panoramic views of Rio de Janeiro, including the beaches, mountains, and cityscape. The statue can be reached by train, car, or hiking trails, and the site is popular for photography and sunset visits.

Rio de Janeiro Beaches

Rio’s beaches, especially Copacabana and Ipanema, are world famous. They are not only great for swimming and sunbathing but also for sports such as beach volleyball and surfing. The beaches are full of vendors selling snacks, drinks, and souvenirs, and they often host cultural events and festivals. Visiting the beaches gives a real sense of the social and cultural life of Rio.

Amazon Rainforest

The Amazon Rainforest is the largest tropical rainforest in the world, covering much of northern Brazil. It is home to an incredible variety of plants and animals, including jaguars, macaws, and river dolphins. Visitors can explore the rainforest by guided tours, jungle lodges, and river cruises. Indigenous communities live in the region, offering insight into traditional ways of life. The Amazon is also important for its ecological role in producing oxygen and regulating the climate.

Iguaçu Falls

Iguaçu Falls is a massive waterfall system located on the border between Brazil and Argentina. It has more than 270 individual falls stretching over nearly 3 kilometers. The surrounding rainforest is full of wildlife and walking trails. Visitors can see the falls from multiple viewpoints and even take boat rides near the base of the falls. The sound and power of the water make it a breathtaking natural wonder.

Sugarloaf Mountain

Sugarloaf Mountain is a tall granite peak at the mouth of Guanabara Bay in Rio. Visitors reach the top using a famous cable car system that offers 360-degree views of the city, beaches, and ocean. It is an especially popular spot at sunset when the city is bathed in golden light. Hiking trails are also available for those who enjoy a physical challenge combined with scenic views.

Salvador Historic Center

Pelourinho is the historic center of Salvador, known for its colorful colonial buildings, cobblestone streets, and vibrant culture. It was once a center of the African slave trade, and today it preserves Afro-Brazilian traditions through music, dance, and religion. Visitors can enjoy live performances, museums, local food, and festivals like Carnival. It is a place where history and culture come alive.

Fernando de Noronha

Fernando de Noronha is an archipelago of 21 islands in the Atlantic Ocean, famous for its natural beauty and biodiversity. The waters are crystal clear, making it ideal for snorkeling, scuba diving, and surfing. The islands are home to sea turtles, dolphins, and tropical birds. Strict environmental protections limit the number of visitors, preserving the islands’ pristine beaches and coral reefs. It is considered one of the most beautiful and exclusive destinations in Brazil.

General Tips

Portuguese is the main language, so learning common phrases makes communication easier. Keep valuables hidden and be cautious in crowded areas. Use bottled water and avoid drinking tap water in some places. Wear sunscreen and lightweight clothing because of the warm climate. Plan extra time for travel since traffic can be heavy in large cities.
